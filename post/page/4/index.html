The catch-up dilemma

Mon, Jan 1, 0001

I eventually had to stop telling people that I studied obesity. Backyard barbeques, grocery stores, a friend’s house, it didn’t matter. It was always the same question. “How do I lose weight?” Again, and again, and again. It’s not the question I find depressing; it’s the answer. If you want to lose weight, and keep it off, you have to radically change how you live your life. Nobody wants to hear that.

What is overweight, really?

Mon, Jan 1, 0001

The image below is from the WHO, and I don’t like it. The top figure represents both overweight and obese people. According to the WHO, a person with a body mass index (BMI) of 25 or greater is overweight, while a person with a BMI of 30 or more is obese. The WHO arrived at these numbers partly due to research comparing health outcomes across people with different BMIs. These studies produce graphs like this one, which looks at mortality rate as a function of BMI.

Where “Hunter-Gatherer Energetics and Human Obesity” gets it wrong

Mon, Jan 1, 0001

There is a recent PLoS ONE article on obesity getting quite a bit of attention. The article, Hunter-Gatherer Energetics and Human Obesity, reports research looking at total energy expenditure in a sample of adults from a Tanzanian hunter-gatherer tribe, the Hadza. Given their “traditional physically active lifestyle” the authors expected that Hadza to have lower body fat and to expend more energy than modern, Western populations, and the aim the research was to see if this was in fact true.
